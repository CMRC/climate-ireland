Adaptation & Uncertainty

Introduction

Uncertainty describes the quality of our knowledge of risk. Risk is commonly defined as the product of the probability or likelihood of occurrence of a hazard and the magnitude of consequence. As a result, uncertainty may relate to either the probability or consequence components of risk [1].

All decisions made are intended to bring some future benefit to something or someone and without uncertainty, these decisions would be straightforward. However, in reality, making decisions is far more complex and generally involves judgements regarding uncertainty. In order to overcome uncertainties and make well informed decisions, it is important to: (1) identify the sources of uncertainty, (2) understand how these uncertainties contribute to the decision uncertainty, and (3) manage the uncertainty in the decision making process. [1].

Similar to any other decision context, making adaptation decisions is complex and requires knowledge of future climatic changes. In addition, because adaptation problems do not occur in isolation, difficulties in projecting non-climatic factors such as social, demographic, political and economic change is also problematic. Moreover, in adaptation planning, the end point is not only determined by what is known or anticipated at present but also by what will be experienced and learned as the future unfolds and by policy responses[2].

Adaptation decision-making does not require precise foresight of future change and uncertainties do not prevent anticipatory adaptation. A number of approaches are available to enable decision makers to manage uncertainty and they can be employed at a variety spatial scales, from individual decisions to sector-based and national adaptation planning. In contrast to approaches which seek to develop an optimal plan that may achieve the best results among all possible plans but carries no guarantee beyond a narrowly defined set of circumstances, planning for climate change requires plans based on robustness and flexibility.

  • Robust decision making uses multiple views of the future to support a thorough evaluation of the full range of potential future vulnerabilities and opportunities. On this basis, a plan is developed that is robust across a broad range of possible futures but may not necessarily perform optimally in any single future. Robust decision making aims to avoid situations in which the plan would fail to meet it's goals and clearly identifies the remaining vulnerabilities under which the plan would fail to meet its goals[walker]. For example, instead of planning for a dam that will have a 100 year lifespan and will full fill its purpose under a set of prescribed set of conditions, a robust strategy identifies the full range of possible futures and then seeks strategies that will perform well over a wide range of alternative futures.
  • Flexible approaches to adaptation comprise those where decisions are made overtime to continuously adapt while maintaining flexibility about future options. Moreover, flexible approaches encourage robust planning practises that avoid lock-in that happens when policy is placed on an unchanging pathway as the result of a once off adaptation decision that cannot be revisited [2].

Adaptive Management

Adaptive Management is a term which describes the ability to change plans based on new experience or information [3]. Adaptive management calls for managers to take an ecosystem-level view of the resource that they manage and provides a structured approach for developing dynamic and robust adaptation plans to support the implementation of long term plans despite the presence of uncertainties. It sets out a process of continuing phases of assessment, design, implementation and adjustment. The process is often described as 'learning by doing'.

Adaptive management occurs in two phases:

  1. the design phase, in which a dynamic adaptive plan, monitoring programme and pre- and post-implementation actions are designed;
  2. Once the basic plan is established, the plan is implemented and monitoring commences. The process is not linear as monitoring is continuous and may lead to different actions at various points in time.

In developing an adaptive management plan, decision makers follow a number of steps:

  • The existing condition of the area/resource under consideration is first assessed and objectives of the plan are specified;
  • The means by which the objectives are to be achieved are then developed and a basic plan is assembled;
  • Once assembled, the basic plan is assessed in terms of meeting its objectives across the range of plausible futures. The plan is then made more robust (performs well across the range of plausible futures) through four types of actions: shaping actions (actions to reduce failure or enhance success), mitigating actions (actions to reduce the likely adverse effects of a plan), hedging actions (actions to taken to spread or reduce the uncertain adverse affects of the plan) and seizing actions (actions to seize any likely opportunities);
  • Mechanisms to monitor the plan over time are then identified. This called contingency planning. Signposts specify information that should be tracked in order to determine if the plan is meeting its objectives. Critical values of signposts (triggers) beyond which additional actions should be taken are identified.

The adaptive management plan is then implemented and monitored over time. The plan is evaluated based on the status of the resource under consideration, indicated by signposts, and adjusted to reflect both lessons learned in the management process and also the development of new information. Inherent in this design is a need for continuous data gathering and a commitment to following the process.

Adaptation Pathways and Adaptation Tipping Points

In developing an adaptation plan, there is a need to consider timing of actions and to and take a more flexible approach where decisions are taken overtime while at the same time maintaining flexibility about future options.

This flexibility can be achieved by sequencing the implementation of different measures over time so that options are available to deal with a range of possible futures. For example, low-regret options are taken earlier and more inflexible measures, e.g. grey options, are delayed in anticipation of better information [5]. Moreover, this approach aims to ensure that whatever measures are taken in the short to medium term are set within a framework that will not be maladaptive should climate change progress in a way that is not yet anticipated as being the most probable.

Central to adaptation pathways are Adaptation Tipping Points (ATPs), which are the conditions under which an action no longer meets its objective and after reaching a tipping point, additional actions are needed. The adaptation pathways approach presents a sequence of possible actions after a tipping point forming an adaptation tree [6]

On this basis, decision makers need to design dynamic adaptive plans that contain a strategic vision of the future, commit to short and medium term actions, and establish a plan to guide future actions, meet changing circumstances and ensure resilience in the long term. With the adaptation map, decision makers can identify opportunities, low regret measures, lock-ins and the timing of actions in order to support decision making in a changing environment [6].

Dynamic Adaptation Pathways

Both the adaptation management and adaptation pathways approaches aim at supporting decision-making in the long term and in the face of uncertainty and emphasise the need for adaptability of plans. More specifically, both approaches offer support in choosing near term actions while keeping open the possibility to modify extend or otherwise alter the plan in response to how the future unfolds. The combination of Adaptive Management and Adaptation Pathways is termed 'Dynamic Adaptive Policy Pathways'. This approach includes transient scenarios representing a variety of uncertainties and their development overtime different types of actions to hand vulnerabilities and opportunities; the sequencing of adaptation actions; and a monitoring system with related contingency actions to keep the plan on track to a preferred pathway [6].

  1. The first step involves describing the system, objectives constraints in the current and possible future situations. In addition, a description of the uncertainties involved in the decision making process should be identified and are not restricted to uncertainties about the future but also about data or models that are being used;
  2. The second step involves the development of future scenarios based on the uncertainties identified in the first step. A comparison of the current situation and possible future situations can they be made and any vulnerabilities or opportunities that may arise are identified. This determines if and when actions are needed;
  3. To assemble a rich set of actions, these can be classified as shaping actions (actions to reduce failure or enhance success), mitigating actions (actions to reduce the likely adverse effects of a plan), hedging actions (actions to taken to spread or reduce the uncertain adverse affects of the plan) and seizing actions (actions to seize any likely opportunities). These actions are then used to assemble adaptation pathways and the performance of each adaptation pathway is assessed in the light of the defined objectives to determine its adaptation tipping point.
  4. Once a set of actions seem adequate, potential pathways can be constructed and one or more pathways can then be selected as input for a dynamic robust adaptation plan. The aim of the plan is to keep the preferred pathway open as long as possible. For this purpose, contingency actions are specified and a trigger for each contingency is specified and monitored [7].
