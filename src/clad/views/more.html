What is Climate Change?

Climate refers to global or regional weather patterns experienced over long time periods, typically 30 years. Throughout the earth's history, natural and human factors have affected global climate. Examples of natural changes in climate include changes in the earth's orbit and volcanic eruptions while humans influence global climate through the release of greenhouse gases, such as carbon dioxide, into the atmosphere. Over the last 100 years, climate is changing and in its fourth assessment report, the IPCC conclude that there is 90% probability that the global warming of the last 50 years is due to man-made green house gases. Global warming is now taking place at a much faster rate than previously expected and poses significant and imminent threat to Ireland. Similar to global patterns of climate change, the last century has been a period of rapid climate change for Ireland. There has been an increase in average annual temperature of 0.5°C during the 20th century with a more rapid rate of increase evident since the 1980s while patterns of precipitation have also changed, increased levels of precipitation are now evident in the north and west of the country while decreases or smaller increases in levels of precipitation are recorded in the south and east. These observed changes have had already had impacts on many parts of society and it is expected that over the coming century, these changes will continue and intensify.

Evidence for Climate Change

The global climate is changing and this is particularly the case since the onset of the industrial period and its associated rapid increase in levels of emission of greenhouse gas emissions, CO2 in particular. From 1906-2005, average global temperature has increased by 0.74°C and similar to the global picture, for Ireland, measurements indicates an increase in average temperature of 0.5°C during the 20th century and the rate of increase has become more rapid since the 1980s. This increase in temperatures is reflected by the fact that, in the northern hemisphere, the 1990s was the hottest decade of what has been the hottest century for a millennia. Globally, an increase in temperature will cause sea levels to rise and will change patterns of precipitation. Other likely effects of increasing temperatures include the more frequent occurrence of extreme events such as droughts, heat waves, heavy rainfall events, species extinction and change in agricultural yields. The rate of warming and the related changes will vary from region to region. However, the exact nature of these regional changes remains uncertain.

Why is Climate Change Serious?

Climate Modelling

The climate system can be represented by computer models which comprise a range of mathematical equations. These computer models allow us to predict how the climate might change in the future. Recent developments in climate modelling, in addition to improved computing power, allow us to have greater confidence in our predictions. However, developing climate models is a complex procedure and climate models cannot fully represent the complexity of our climate system. As a result, there is still considerable uncertainty associated with predicted climate information. Due to the complexities of developing climate models, there are only a few places in the world that carry out climate modelling. In order to create a global climate model (GCM), the globe is divided into grid blocks or cell. These grid blocks will normally have a coarse resolution, covering 3-4 degrees across the globe. The model is then run and predictions are computed for each grid block on the globe. Using a variety of complex mathematical procedures, these global projections can be down-scaled using a regional climate model (RCM) which will provide predictions for smaller areas of land or sea.

Adaptation and Mitigation

Climate change is happening and even if levels of greenhouse gas emissions decrease rapidly, there will still be some inevitable effects of climate change. This is due to our history of greenhouse gas emission, the persistence of these gases in the atmosphere and the slow warming of the oceans. The slow response of the oceans mean that temperatures and sea level will continue to rise for several decades regardless of our efforts to reduce emissions of greenhouse gases. Our efforts to reduce the levels of greenhouse gas emission in order to prevent the worst effects of climate change are known as mitigating actions. However, in order to protect from the now inevitable consequences of climate change, adaptation actions are essential. The IPCC defines adaptation as: "adjustments in natural or human systems in response to actual or expected climatic stimuli and their effects, which moderate harm or exploit beneficial opportunities". In order to adjust to and to take advantage of the consequences of climate change, it is essential that we build our capacity to adapt (adaptive capacity).

Projections for Ireland

Observed changes in Ireland's climate over the last century are in line with global and regional trends associated with human induced climate change. Over the coming centuries, these changes are expected to continue and increase.

  • Temperatures are expected to increase by 1-3°C by the end of the century while the frequency of heat waves are expected to increase and the frequency of cold snaps are expected to decrease.
  • Winters are expected to be wetter in the west of Ireland while summers are expected to be drier in the southeast of Ireland (5-25% less rainfall in 2021 to 2060 compared to 1961-2000).
  • Storm frequency is expected to decrease but the intensity of these storms if expected is increase. Storm tracks are also projected to shift northwards.
  • Sea level is projected to rise by up to 60 cm by the end of the century and these changes are expected to magnify the impacts of changing storm surge and wave patterns in coastal areas.

Coastal System

Climate Change and Coasts

Coastal areas are exposed to a wide range of climate change impacts which affect both the terrestrial and marine environment, the economy and society. For example, the life and economy of coastal communities (fishing, transport, agriculture, tourism) can be impacted upon by changes in: sea level and temperature, winds, waves and surges, the abundance and migration of commercial species of fish, biodiversity and the natural beauty of the area. In addition, coastal land use and development depends upon the availability of land for both human activity and natural habitats which can be impacted by sea level rise, erosion and flooding.

Changes in climate may have both physical and socio-economic impacts.

Physical impacts: changes in air and surface sea temperature, precipitation regime and weather patterns; sea level rise, storminess, coastal squeeze and changing water salinity; changing ecosystems at lands and sea (including fish stocks); altering regime and intensity of floods, and extreme events.

Socio-economic impacts: resources available for economy and development, safety of existing buildings and infrastructure and spending on their maintenance, perspectives of new developments, attractiveness of a territory as a place for life and business, insurance policies and payments.

Impacts do not only cause negative effects.

Appropriate adaptation to new climate conditions based on strategic planning aims to harness new opportunities and foresee and reduce risks for existing economy and environment.

Sea surface temperature

Water has a higher capacity than air to uptake and retain heat and as a result the oceans uptake 20 times more heat than the atmosphere. In general, changes in sea temperature follow those of global air temperature and over the period 1961-2003 the average global temperature of the oceans has risen by 0.10Â°C down to a depth of 700m. According to the IPCC, this warming is to continue and by the end of the 21st century global ocean temperatures will have increased by an average of 2-3°C. Importantly, this warming results in the thermal expansion of sea waters which in combination with the related melting of the ice caps is seen as the primary cause of global sea level rise. Changes in sea surface temperature may also significantly influence the global circulation of water masses and currents which are driven by differences in the relative densities of warm,and cold, saline and fresh waters (also known as thermohaline circulation). Sea temperature also has an impact on atmospheric processes and can result in the increasing intensity and frequency of cyclones which bring severe winds and storms. Warming sea water also has an influence on ecosystems, the pole-ward migration of species, mortality, coral bleaching and increased algae blooms.

Sea Level Rise

Over the last century sea level has risen globally by 1-2 mm/yr, at a far greater rate than was observed for the 19th century. It is predicted that in the 21st century this process will continue and at a faster rate with projections suggesting a rise in global sea levels in the range of 9 - 88 mm by 2100. The melting of ice cover in a warmer climate, in combination with the thermal expansion of seawater due to higher water temperature, is seen as the most important factor in causing sea level rise. However, the actual increase in sea level will vary significantly for different locations as well as the potential damage it may cause. Natural elevation of certain areas due to global geological processes may compensate for rising sea level. Nonetheless, highly populated estuarine areas with developed economies and infrastructure are facing the biggest risk.

Rising sea level are associated with the physical loss of lands (or coastal squeeze), increased intensity of storms and floods, coastal erosion, changing exploitation conditions for marine and coastal infrastructure and equipment used for fishing, aquaculture, ports, gas and oil terminals. On the other hand, increasing sea level may bring new opportunities such as better accessibility to ports, additional recreational facilities and resources for marine energy.

Water Salinity

Warming air temperature, global water and air circulation affect the chemical and physical characteristics of sea water. Observations show that water in the lower tropical and sub-tropical latitudes becomes more saline as the result of higher temperatures and rates of evaporation. At the same time waters at higher sub-polar latitudes become fresher due to surface run-off, more intense precipitation and the melting of ice covers. In general, Pacific waters become less saline while Atlantic and Indian oceans show increases in levels of salinity. Such shifts may affect ecosystem balance through changes in the living environment and food chains as well as global water circulation which is partly driven by differences between the relative densities of saline and fresh waters.

See impacts of Water Salinity and Acidification on Irish coasts.

Ocean Acidification

As a result of increased anthropogenic emissions of CO2, an increased amount of atmospheric CO2 is being absorbed by the oceans, a process termed "ocean acidification", which alters the content of inorganic carbon in sea water and results in a decrease in levels pH. Fossil fuel combustion, agricultural activity and surface water run off also contribute to increased levels of acidity. The impact of these factors is small at the global level but on a local scale, where acidic inputs are concentrated, the effects may be notable. Observed changes in average global levels of pH for sea water show a decrease from 8.2 to 8.1 which represent a 30% increase in levels of acidity. Observations show that the lowest rate of increase in acidity (decreased pH) has occurred in the low latitudes while the highest levels of acidification are apparent in the high latitudes which can be linked to higher buffering capacities of the tropical waters. The IPCC "business as usual" scenario suggests a further increase in acidity by up to 120% by 2100 which would result in conditions that have not been present on Earth for millions of years. Due to the lack of historical observation for changes of such a scale, the full range of impacts continued ocean acidification might result in are difficult to predict. However, ocean acidification has been predicted to result in substantial ecosystem changes, in particular the ability of organisms to produce calcium necessary for shells and skeletons, resulting in coral bleaching, and the migration or extinction of some forms of sea life which form the basis of all marine food webs - effects that have already been witnessed in some areas. Ocean acidification is therefore one of the key long-term threats for the fishing industry, with knock-on effects for the livelihoods of coastal communities.

See impacts of Water Salinity and Acidification on Irish coasts.

Coastal Erosion

Rising Sea Level may cause the physical loss of land along the shore and affects the distribution of sediments and rocky materials between the land and near-shore (littoral) zone. More severe storminess and wave height makes these redistributive processes more rapid and intense, potentially causing coastal erosion. However, the intensity and speed of the erosion depends on a range of local characteristics, such as the type of sediments (soft sand or solid rocks), the shape of the coast, and the local wave and wind regime which form the conditions for transfer and deposit of eroded materials. Some of these factors may be unknown, hardly predictable or require long-term monitoring, resulting in difficulties in deciding upon an appropriate management response. Moreover, a gradual increase in impacts may cause an abrupt change if a critical threshold is crossed, triggering the geomorphologic system to shift out of a relatively settled state and into one of rapid change. This period of change may persist until a new 'semi-equilibrium' coastal form can be reached that is in step with the new environmental regime. Although coastal erosion is a natural adaptation process, it can have crucial consequences for human-made environments along the shore, including settlements and infrastructure, and may cause damage to densely populated economically active coasts around the globe.

Coastal Squeeze

Change and retreat of shore line due to sea level rise and erosion is a natural process of adaptation for ecosystems and coastal geomorphology. In natural conditions it usually results in the migration of coastal habitats further inland without major damage to ecosystems. However human-made coastal defence structures such as sea walls and infrastructure situated along the coast provide a barrier to landward ecosystem migration. As a result, habitats become "trapped" between the rising sea and unmoving constructions, causing a drastic reduction of space for salt marshes, dunes and other ecologically critical natural habitats. This process - known as "coastal squeeze" - significantly affects human activities as a result of the loss of lands and habitats, including reduced areas for recreational use and natural buffers against the impacts of storms and floods. Although it is difficult to attribute coastal erosion and coastal squeeze specifically to climate change, it is highly likely that sea level rise and the increasing intensity of storms may significantly accelerate rates of coastal erosion. In turn, an increase in the construction of hard defences aiming to protect the land from immediate effects of sea level rise and erosion may amplify coastal squeeze.

Floods and Extreme Events

In future, extreme climate events may become more prevalent and severe. Moreover, their geographical distribution may change, endangering areas where such extreme conditions were previously rare or even unheard of. If such areas are densely populated or home to important infrastructure, unexpected extreme events can result in significant losses to life, property and habitats.

Typhoons, storms, flooding, severe weather conditions and other extreme events result from a combination of factors including coastal geomorphology, local and global atmospheric conditions and long-term weather patterns. Climate change may affect these conditions in various, complex and often unpredictable ways. Usually extreme conditions result from the combination of several effects, for example severe winds, heavy precipitation and low air pressure can result in extreme coastal inundation due to the effects of storm surge and terrestrial surface flooding. Sea level rise brings higher tides and wave height causing additional increase of water level. Thus, climate change are likely to result in more severe, more often and less predictable floods.

Ecosystems

Complex ocean and coastal ecosystem are highly sensitive to various impact of climate change including sea water characteristics (sea temperature, salinity, acidity), coastal geomorphology and environment (sea level rise, erosion, coastal squeeze), physical impacts (intensity of storms and waves, currents, migration of water masses) and others. Moreover, adaptation processes in human and natural systems (change in land use and land availability, invasion of new species) may cause significant change in native ecosystems. In some cases this process will lead to gradual natural adaptation to new conditions, in others may trigger dramatic changes effecting both environment and economy. Increase in the amount of CO2 absorbed by sea water will likely lead to higher rates of photosynthesis increasing productivity of aquatic vegetation. This may cause range of impacts from positive (increased nutrient base for upper organisms) to negative (spreading coastal vegetation, water pollution). Higher level of acidity associated with CO2 may also affect calcifying organisms such as molluscs and corals. Corals are also affected by increasing storms and tropical cyclones which may cause massive damage of the reefs. Together with other human impacts, such as overfishing and water pollution, climate change represents significant risk for coral reefs ecosystems. Distribution, reproduction and migration of marine organisms may be affected in a complex way. Alongside with possible shift of the ecosystems northward, impacts of climate change may affect food chains at different levels which may have hardly predictable results on human activities such as fisheries. Coastal and estuarine shallow water ecosystems are likely to be more vulnerable to changes. Except of marine organisms, terrestrial species may be affected as well. For example, overwintering of migratory birds on the British coast has been observed as the response to temperature rise.

Salinity and Acidification

Projections: The salinity of Irish surface and intermediate water (up to the depth of 900-1000 m) naturally changes over time following several trends including inflow of waters from different regions (for example Mediterranean) and atmospheric processes from which the North Atlantic Oscillation is especially influential. Storminess and Wave Height

Projections: Several recent studies have indicated that the influence of climate change on Atlantic storm activity will result in increased extreme wind speeds, and an intensification of cyclones. These will contribute to a significant increase in the frequency of storm surges around the entire coast of Ireland for the period 2031-2060, ranging from a 10% increase in frequency in Cork Harbour, to over 30% increases for Sligo Entrance and Clew Bay. Surface wind speeds over the eastern Atlantic are projected to rise marginally in winter. Extreme wave heights have also been predicted to increase by up to 10% in the northwest of Ireland, with no significant change in the south and east.

Risks: Waves are the most critical factor in determining coastal geomorphology. The wave energy regime, in conjunction with coastal lithography determines the rate at which the processes of coastal erosion and accretion will occur. Energy received at the coast (measured in joules/metre) is proportional to a wave height. Therefore, wave height and increasing storminess will increase the risk of erosion in the west and north, which is nevertheless largely offset by typically low erosion rate of the rocky north western shore. Most vulnerable to erosion are the soft-sedimentary eastern and southern coasts, which are likely to receive lower intensity of storms. The most severe and dangerous flood events in Ireland are often those involving coastal inundation through storm surge. Projections of increased storminess also predict additional flood risk to coastal dwellers and critical infrastructure located at the coasts.

Opportunities: Predictions for increasing storminess and wave height are currently seen as an important potential resource for renewable energy, contributing to national energy security and helping the transition towards a low-carbon economy. A projected increase of wind speed in the winter months also provides additional opportunities for wind energy production. However, in order to harness this potential, considerable steps must be taken in technological research and infrastructural development.

Projections: Erosion is caused by a number of factors and many of them such are unique to a specific location. Risks: Until recently, erosion was not considered a significant problem in Ireland as most of the affected areas were sparsely populated.
