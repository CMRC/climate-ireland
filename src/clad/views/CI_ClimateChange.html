Climate Change

The climate of Ireland is changing and understanding how this is happening is an important step in planning for climate change adaptation.

Over the past century, temperatures have increased and this increase has become more rapid since the 1990s, patterns of precipitation are changing, the occurrence of frost and cold days is decreasing while the number of hot days is increasing.

This section of the website will provide you with the information that you need to understand climate change and how projections of possible future climate are developed.

What is Climate Change?

Key Information
  • Climate refers to global and regional weather patterns experienced over long time periods, typically a minimum of 30 years.
  • Over the last 100 years, global mean temperature has risen by 0.74°C and this is attributed to the emission of manmade greenhouse gases.
  • Data indicate that levels of atmospheric greenhouse gases are now 30% higher than at any time during the last 800,000 years and this is enhancing the natural greenhouse effect and warming the planet.

The earth's climate has always been changing however in its fourth assessment report, the IPCC conclude that there is 90% probability that the global warming of the last 50 years is due to man-made green house gases. Global warming is now taking place at a much faster rate than previously expected and poses serious challenges globally and for Ireland.

The Greenhouse Effect

The earth has a natural greenhouse effect and this is due to the absorption of heat radiated from the earth and the lower atmosphere by gases such as water vapour, carbon dioxide and methane. These gases radiate heat back toward the earth's surface. In the absence of this natural effect, the climate of the earth would be far colder than it is today.


Radiation from the sun is reflected off the earth's surface. Some of this radiation passes through the atmosphere to space while some is adsorbed by greenhouse gases and re-radiated back toward the earth

Throughout the Earth's history, natural and human factors have affected global climate. Examples of natural changes in climate include changes in the earth's orbit and volcanic eruptions while humans influence global climate in many ways, one of the most significant being through the release of greenhouse gases, such as carbon dioxide, into the atmosphere. Once emitted to the atmosphere, greenhouse gases absorb thermal radiation and re-radiate it in all directions. Some of this is reflected back towards the surface of the Earth and the lower atmosphere and results in the increase of the average surface temperature of the Earth. This process by which manmade emissions cause an increase in temperature is termed the 'Enhanced Greenhouse Effect'.

Global Warming

The problem of global warming is a result of human activities and, more specifically, to the burning of fossil fuels which releases greenhouse gases into the atmosphere. A range of other activities also contribute to the release of greenhouse gases and these include but are not restricted to agricultural practices, vegetation fires, sewage treatment and the use of synthetic gases in refrigeration and air conditioning. These additional greenhouse gases intensify the natural greenhouse effect and, as a result, warm the planet. The levels of CO2 in the atmosphere in the distant past can be determined from bubbles of air trapped in ice. Data indicate that as a result of human activities, the current concentration of CO2 in the atmosphere is 30% higher than at any time during at least the past 800,000 years and this is enhancing the natural greenhouse effect and accelerating irreversible changes in the climate.

Evidence of Climate Change
Key Information
  • Over the past century, temperatures have increased globally and for Ireland.
  • Sea level is now rising faster than at anytime during the last two to three thousand years.
  • The frequency and intensities of extreme weather events are increasing.
  • The number of frost days is decreasing while the number of warm days is increasing.
  • Places that are normally wet are getting wetter.

Climate is changing on a global basis and this is particularly the case since the onset of the industrial period and the associated rapid increase in levels of emission of greenhouse gases, CO2. Reflecting global patterns of climate change, the last century has been a period of unprecedented climate change in Ireland.

Global Climate Change

  • From 1906-2005, average global sir temperature has increased by 0.74°C and this increase in temperature is demonstrated by the fact that, in the northern hemisphere, the 1990s was the hottest decade of what has been the hottest century for a millennia. The rate of warming and the related changes will vary from region to region. However, the exact nature of these regional changes remains uncertain.

    • Climate Change in Ireland

    • There has been an increase in the mean annual temperature of Ireland of 0.7°C during the 20th century with a more rapid rate of increase evident since the 1980s.
    • The highest ten-yearly rates of increase have occurred since 1980, with a warming rate of 0.042°C per decade.
    • On average, sea level is rising around Ireland at 1mm per year.
    • There has been a decrease in the number of frost days and shortening of the frost season duration.
    • There has been an increase in the number of warm days (temperature > 20°C).
    • There is some evidence of change in patterns of precipitation; increased levels of precipitation are now evident in the north and west of the country while decreases or smaller increases in levels of precipitation are recorded in the south and east.

    These observed changes have had already had impacts on many parts of society,economy and environment and it is expected that over the coming century, these changes will continue and intensify.

Why is Climate Change Serious?
Key Information
  • Observational evidence demonstrates that climate is already changing globally and nationally
  • These changes will continue and intensify in the future


Flooding in Cork city 2009
Climate change poses a significant and imminent threat to Ireland. Climate change is not just an environmental issue, it affects all aspects of life as we know it and its effects will be felt across all sectors.

In the coming century, we can expect, amongst others:

  • higher sea levels;
  • more storms and these will occur with increased intensity;
  • warmer temperatures and warmer waters;
  • and more flooding, summer droughts and changing rainfall patterns.

These changes will put Ireland at risk, both directly and indirectly. For example, storms and violent winds could affect critical infrastructure, summer droughts could put a strain on water supplies, floods could submerge homes and businesses, rising sea levels and increased storm surges could threaten all of Ireland's cities.

Climate Modelling

Key Information
  • Climate models comprise a range of mathematical equations that represent various parts of the Earth's climate system.
  • In projecting future climate, climate models use a wide range of scenarios or storylines to simulate future social, economic, and technological development.
  • Due to the unknowable knowledge, such as future population growth, complexity of the climate system and computational limitations, there is some uncertainty surrounding projections of future climate.
  • Multi-model ensembles of projections provides a means of overcoming these uncertainties and deriving best estimates of possible climate futures.

In developing climate models, scientists are trying to reduce the complex behaviour of the climate to a set of mathematical equations. Climate models are first developed for on a global basis, Global Climate Models (GCMs), and calibrated to simulate past climate. Once the models accurately depict past climate, these models are then used to project how the climate might change in the future. However, in order to employ the outputs of global climate models for local or regional assessments of climate change, GCM outputs are down-scaled using a variety of techniques to provide a higher spatial resolution of the area of interest, this is known as down-scaling of climate models. Nonetheless, developing climate models is a complex procedure and climate models cannot fully represent the complexity of our climate system. As a result, there is still considerable uncertainty associated with projected climate information. However, recent developments in climate modelling, in addition to improved computing power, allow us to have greater confidence in our projections.

Developing Climate Models

Global Climate Models (GCMs) are mathematical representations of the climate system. In order to create a global climate model (GCM), the globe is divided into grid blocks or cell. These grid blocks will normally have a coarse resolution, covering 3-4 degrees across the globe (horizontal grid). The finer the spatial resolution of the horizontal grid, the more spatially detailed the prediction. In a similar way to the horizontal grid, the vertical profile of the atmosphere is divided into a number of different levels. In contrast, to the horizontal grid, the vertical levels are not evenly spaced and simulate atmospheric processes, e.g. cloud formation.

For each horizontal cell and for all vertical levels, GCMs try to simulate as much as possible about the climate system including the incoming and outgoing solar radiation, the way clouds move and precipitation falls, the way ice sheets grow and shrink etc. GCMs also try to simulate how the different parts of the climate system interact with eachother.

In order to parameterise the model for the prediction of the future climate, climate models are first employed to simulate past and current climates and these simulations are compared to actual observations. The models are then tuned, within reasonable boundaries, so that they approximate as closely as possible past and current climates.

Projecting future climate.

Once parameterised to best represent past and current climate conditions, climate models are then employed to project future climate conditions. This is achieved by estimating levels of future emissions of CO2 and other greenhouse gases and using these estimates as inputs to the climate model. This is termed as 'forcing' a climate model. Currently, in forcing climate models to project possible climate futures, two sets of emission scenarios have largely been employed, the Special Report on Emissions Scenarios (SRES) and, more recently, the Representative Concentration Pathways (RCP).

SRES

In order to assess a wide range of possible future emissions levels, using different modelling approaches, quantitative interpretations of what future levels of emission might be are developed and these are determined by predicting the future evolution of the main demographic, economic and technological driving forces of emission levels. These alternative images of how the future greenhouse gas emissions might unfold are termed 'scenarios'. However,it is thought highly unlikely that any single emission path will occur as described in a single scenario. A set of scenarios, each making different assumptions for greenhouse gas emissions, has been developed to represent the range of driving forces and emissions so as to best reflect current understanding and knowledge about an uncertain future.

To add context to the scenarios, each scenario is organised into four different narrative storylines or scenario families which contain scenarios that are similar to eachother in some respects.

As a result, for each storyline, several different scenarios were developed using different modelling approaches to examine the range of outcomes arising from a range of models that use similar assumptions about driving forces. A multi model approach encompasses the current range of uncertainty surrounding future levels of emissions arising from the different characteristics of these models, in addition to uncertainties that arise from scenario driving forces such as demographic, social and economic, and broad range of technological developments that drive these models

On this basis, four narrative storylines were developed labelled A1, A2, B1 and B2. In simple terms, the four storylines combine two sets of divergent tendencies: one set varying between strong economic and environmental values, the other between increasing globalisation and increasing regionalisation.


The four IPCC SRES narrative story lines have been developed according to two sets of divergent tendencies, one varying between strong economic and environmental values and the other between increasing globalisation and increasing regionalisation.
  • The A1 storyline and family of scenarios represent a future world of very rapid economic growth, global population that peaks mid-century and declines thereafter and the rapid introduction of new and more efficient technologies. This story line also develops into three groups that describe alternatives in the development of energy supply: fossil fuel intensive (A1F1), non-fossil fuel energy sources (A1T) or a balance across all sources (A1B)
  • The A2 storyline and family of scenarios is representative of a heterogeneous world with continuously increasing population and regionally orientated economic growth that is more fragmented and slower than in the other storylines.
  • The B1 storyline and scenario family represents a convergent world with the same global population as the A1 scenario but with rapid changes in economic structures towards a service and information economy, with reductions in material intensity and the introduction of clean resource-efficient technologies.
  • The B2 storyline and scenario family represents a world in which the emphasis is on local solutions to economic, social and environmental sustainability with continuously increasing population, albeit at a lower rate than A2, and intermediate economic development.

    • On the basis of the four narrative storylines, projected climates are computed for each grid block on the globe.

    RCPs

    The Representative Concentration Pathways (RCP) are based on selected scenarios from four modelling teams working on integrated assessment modelling, climate modelling and modelling and analysis of impacts and each of the RCPs cover the period up to 2100. The RCPs differ from the SRES as they refer to the atmospheric concentration of greenhouse gases as defined by their total radiative forcing (cumulative measure of human emission from all sources expressed in Watts per square metre) rather than levels of emission of greenhouse gases. Moreover, each single RCP cannot be associated with a set of social, economic and technological assumptions. This is because any single radiative forcing pathway can result from a diverse range of socioeconomic and technological development scenarios.

    In conducting climate modelling with RCPs, four RCPs are employed:

    • RCP 8.5: Rising radiative forcing pathway leading to 8.5/W/m2 in 2100.
    • RCP 6: Stabilisation without overshoot pathway to 6W/m2 at stabilisation after 2100
    • RCP 4.5: Stabilisation without overshoot pathway to 4.5 W/m2 at stabilisation after 2100.
    • RCP 3-PD2: Peak in radiative forcing at ~3 W/m2 before 2100 and decline.

     

    Down-scaling climate models for Ireland

    Despite the sophistication and complexity of GCMs, generally the output from these models is too course to be effectively employed in assessing regional or local impacts. This is because important variations occur due to processes operating at sub GCM grid scale. Due to computational requirements, the grid box output for GCMs are generally in the order of 100s km. In order to overcome these issues, regionalisation or down-scaling of GCM outputs is required.


    Regional climate models (RCM) are nested within a global climate model (GCM). Due to the computational requirements of developing climate projections for the entire globe, the spatial resolution of the GCM is relatively large. RCMs model climate for limited areas and as a result, the spatial resolution of the model is relatively high compared to GCMs and information such as detailed topography can be included.

    For Ireland, Down-scaling of GCMs to regional scale has been achieved using two approaches: 1)by statistically downscaling the outputs of a GCM; 2) by dynamical down-scaling which fits outputs from GCM into a regional climate model (RCM).

    Statistical down-scaling

    The statistical down-scaling approach converts GCM/global-scale outputs to regional scale conditions. This is achieved by applying information from GCMs to a region by linking available climate observations to hindcasted GCM outputs. On this basis, a predictive model is developed which is trained to match, as closely as possible, climate observations.

    Advantages of this approach include less computational effort than dynamical down-scaling, the provision of fine scale information providing there is a dense network of observation stations, and the employment of ensemble GCM results rather than a single GCM.

    Dynamical down-scaling

    Dynamical down-scaling fits outputs from GCMs into a Regional Climate Model (RCM). The RCM is similar to a GCM but its spatial extent is limited to a portion of the globe. As a result, RCM can be considered to be nested within a GCM and simulate the effects of climate change at sub-GCM grid squares. Moreover, RCMs use the lateral meteorological boundary conditions of the GCM and include highly resolved geographic information such as complex topography and coastlines. This allows for an improved climatic simulation for limited spatial areas, e.g. regional level, than would be provided for by GCMs.

    Dynamical down-scaling has a number of advantages and these include not being limited by available observations, full model outputs which can be used to drive applications requiring highly resolved climate data, and the potential for capturing meso-scale non-linear effects and providing coherent information among multiple climate variables

    Sources of Uncertainty in Climate Model Projections

    Modelling future climate is a complex procedure and although there have been large advances in modelling chaotic behaviour and natural variability in the climate system, it is not an exact science and it is recognised that climate models will not be able to provide a singular projection of future climate.

    1. Unknowable knowledge

      The projections produced by climate models are based on future assumptions of socio-economic conditions, technological change and future population growth and their associated estimate of CO2 emissions. In order to take these uncertainties into account, a range of future storylines detailing socio-economic conditions, technological change and future population growth are employed and these are termed scenarios.

    2. Uncertainty in model configuration.

      The configuration of climate models to represent the climate system is an important step and is based on both scientific and computational considerations. For example, choices include the number of vertical and horizontal resolutions, physics parameterisation schemes, values of these parameters and numerical algorithms. As a result, different climate models will simulate different climatic responses to the same scenario. As we move to Regional Climate Models, uncertainty in climate model configuration increases.

      In order to overcome uncertainty in model configuration, a wide range or ensemble of climate models are employed, to provide a projection of an average climate.

    3. Uncertainty due to the random nature of natural forcing.

      The climate system and its natural forcing are inherently stochastic or random. The nature of future natural forcing are unknown and although these are less important than the uncertainty associated with manmade forcing, these still add uncertainty to the projection of future climate.

Projections of Global Climate Change

International scientific evidence has shown that human activities have begun to modify global climate and that these changes will have significant social and economic implications. The Fourth Assessment Report of the IPCC (2007) details projections from a range of global climate models and indicate that significant impacts on world climate are likely to occur over the present century. The projected increase in global temperatures are unlikely to be uniformly distributed with increased rates of warming, nearly double the global average, projected to occur at higher latitudes. This projected regional variation in global warming will also affect the distribution and rate of change in other meteorological variables.

  • Global temperatures are projected to increase by between 1.8°C to 4.0°C by 2080 - 2099.
  • An increase in the occurrence of hot extremes, heat waves and heavy precipitation events is likely to occur.
  • An increase in levels of precipitation is projected for the mid to high latitudes with reductions projected for the low latitudes.
  • A widespread decrease in mountain glaciers is expected while snow cover is also projected to contract and the upper layers of permafrost are projected to melt.
  • Antarctica is to gain mass due to enhanced snowfall. In contrast, Greenland is likely to lose mass due a greater relative increase in runoff. The extent of summer sea ice cover in the Arctic and Antarctica is projected to decrease and, according to some model predictions, summer sea ice in the Arctic is expected to disappear towards the end of this century.
  • Globally, sea level is expected to rise by between 0.28 m and 0.43 m by the end of the century.
  • Storm track locations are projected to shift polewards while the number of tropical cyclones per year is likely to decrease. However, the intensity of these storms is expected to increase.
  • The Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation (MOC), of which the Gulf Stream is a part, is estimated to reduce by 25% during the present century. However, an abrupt transition during this period is unlikely.

Projections of Climate Change for Ireland

Observed changes in Ireland's climate over the last century are in line with global and regional trends associated with human induced climate change. Over the coming centuries, these changes are expected to continue and increase. Based on existing down-scaled climate projection data for Ireland (Sweeney et al 2008; McGrath et al. 2008; Nolan et al. 2012a; 2012b), the following climatic changes can be expected:

  • Temperatures are expected to increase everywhere relative to the present and by the end of the century, summer and autumn temperatures are expected to increase by 1-3°C.
  • Increasing temperatures are expected to bring a change in the frequency and intensity of extreme events. An increase in the duration of heat waves is expected while the frequency of cold snaps is likely to decrease.
  • Projections for changes in precipitation are more uncertain than temperature. However, a robust signal of increased seasonality is evident and winters are expected to be wetter, particularly in the west, while summers are expected to be drier, particularly in the southeast of Ireland.
  • As changes in average climate progress, with the exception of cold snaps, changes in the occurrence and magnitude of extreme events can be expected.
  • Increases in wind speed can be expected and increases in 60 m average wind speeds of 8-11% are likely by mid-century. However, these estimates remain subject to high levels of uncertainty.
  • Changing patterns of precipitation and evapotranspiration will affect streamflow. Increases of up to 20% are projected for winter streamflow by the mid to late century while reductions of up to 40% are expected for summer in many catchments. However, due to differing catchment characteristics, different signatures of change are expected.
  • Sea level is projected to rise by up to 60 cm by the end of the century and these changes are expected to magnify the impacts of changing storm surge and wave patterns in coastal areas. However, locally, rises will vary dependent upon rates of isostatic rebound and topography.
  • For many coastal cities and key infrastructure, flood risk will become an even more serious issue than at present. This is due to a combination of the increased risk of both storm surges and river flooding.
